The water cycle describes the continuous movement of water within the Earth and atmosphere. Evaporation occurs when the sun heats surface water, converting it into water vapor that rises into the atmosphere. As the vapor cools at higher altitudes, it condenses around small particles to form clouds. When enough water accumulates in clouds, precipitation falls in the form of rain, snow, sleet, or hail. Water collects in oceans, rivers, and lakes, or infiltrates the soil to replenish groundwater reserves. The cycle has no distinct beginning or end and operates continuously across the planet.
The Amazon River is the largest river in the world by discharge volume of water. It originates in the Andes Mountains of Peru and flows approximately 6,400 kilometers eastward through Brazil before emptying into the Atlantic Ocean. The river drains an area of about 7 million square kilometers, representing roughly 40 percent of South America. During the wet season, the river can reach widths of 48 kilometers or more in some locations. The Amazon basin contains the largest tropical rainforest on Earth and holds an estimated 20 percent of the world's fresh water.
Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The process occurs primarily in the chloroplasts of plant cells, where chlorophyll absorbs sunlight. Carbon dioxide from the air and water from the soil are the raw materials. The overall reaction produces glucose and oxygen as byproducts. In the light-dependent reactions, light energy is used to split water molecules and generate ATP. In the light-independent reactions, also called the Calvin cycle, carbon dioxide is fixed into organic compounds using the ATP produced earlier.
The Great Wall of China is a series of fortifications built across the northern borders of ancient Chinese states. Construction began as early as the 7th century BCE, with various states building walls to protect their territories. The most well-known sections were built during the Ming dynasty between 1368 and 1644. The wall stretches approximately 21,196 kilometers in total, including all its branches. It was constructed from stone, brick, tamped earth, wood, and other materials, varying by region and available resources. Watchtowers, garrison stations, and signaling capabilities were integrated into the structure.
The human skeletal system consists of 206 bones in adults, though infants are born with around 270 to 300 bones that fuse over time. Bones serve multiple functions including structural support, protection of internal organs, attachment points for muscles, production of blood cells in the marrow, and storage of minerals such as calcium and phosphorus. Bone tissue is classified as either compact or spongy. Compact bone forms the dense outer layer, while spongy bone fills the interior with a lattice of trabeculae. Joints connect bones and allow varying degrees of movement depending on their type.
The speed of light in a vacuum is approximately 299,792 kilometers per second. This constant, denoted by the letter c, plays a central role in physics and is the maximum speed at which all conventional matter and information can travel. It was first measured with reasonable accuracy in the 17th century by Ole Rømer using observations of Jupiter's moons. The exact value was established through increasingly precise experiments over the following centuries. In 1983, the meter was officially redefined in terms of the speed of light, fixing c at exactly 299,792,458 meters per second.
Plate tectonics is the scientific theory that describes the large-scale motion of the plates that make up Earth's lithosphere. There are approximately 15 to 20 major tectonic plates. These rigid segments move relative to one another at rates of a few centimeters per year. Boundaries between plates are of three types: convergent, where plates move toward each other; divergent, where plates move apart; and transform, where plates slide laterally past each other. These movements are responsible for the formation of mountain ranges, ocean trenches, volcanic arcs, and mid-ocean ridges, as well as most earthquakes.
The Gutenberg printing press, developed by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440, introduced mechanical movable type printing to Europe. The press allowed for the mass production of books and other printed materials, significantly reducing the cost and time required to produce them. Before this invention, books were typically copied by hand. Within decades of its introduction, hundreds of thousands of books had been printed across Europe. The technology contributed to the spread of literacy, standardization of languages, and wider distribution of scientific and religious texts throughout the continent.
Mitosis is the process by which a single eukaryotic cell divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells. The process is divided into several phases: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, followed by cytokinesis. During prophase, chromosomes condense and become visible. In metaphase, chromosomes align along the cell's equatorial plane. Anaphase involves the separation of sister chromatids to opposite poles of the cell. Telophase sees the formation of two new nuclei. Cytokinesis then divides the cytoplasm, completing the production of two daughter cells with identical genetic material.
The Sahara Desert is the largest hot desert in the world, covering approximately 9.2 million square kilometers across northern Africa. It spans 11 countries and is bordered by the Atlantic Ocean to the west, the Atlas Mountains and Mediterranean Sea to the north, the Red Sea to the east, and the Sahel to the south. Despite its reputation as an arid wasteland, the Sahara contains diverse landscapes including sand dunes, rocky plateaus, gravel plains, mountains, and oasis valleys. Temperatures can exceed 50 degrees Celsius during the day and drop below freezing at night in some areas.
The periodic table organizes all known chemical elements in order of increasing atomic number. It was first published by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869. Elements are arranged in rows called periods and columns called groups or families. Elements in the same group share similar chemical properties because they have the same number of electrons in their outermost shell. The table currently contains 118 confirmed elements, with 94 occurring naturally on Earth. The remaining elements have been synthesized in laboratories. Metals, nonmetals, and metalloids are broadly distributed across the table in recognizable regions.
The International Space Station is a modular space station in low Earth orbit. It is a multinational project involving five space agencies: NASA, Roscosmos, JAXA, ESA, and CSA. Construction began in 1998 and was declared complete in 2011, though subsequent additions have been made. The station orbits at an altitude of approximately 400 kilometers and completes one orbit of Earth approximately every 90 minutes. It has been continuously occupied since November 2000. The station serves as a laboratory for scientific research in fields including biology, human physiology, physics, astronomy, and meteorology.
Binary code is a system of representing text, computer processor instructions, or any other data using a two-symbol system. The two symbols are typically 0 and 1. Modern computers and electronic devices use binary code as the fundamental system for data representation at the hardware level. Each 0 or 1 is called a bit, and eight bits form a byte. Binary numbers can represent any integer; for example, the binary number 1010 corresponds to the decimal number 10. All modern digital data, including images, audio, and video, is ultimately stored and processed as sequences of binary digits.
Coral reefs are underwater ecosystems built by colonies of tiny animals called coral polyps. These polyps secrete calcium carbonate to form hard skeletons that accumulate over time to create reef structures. Coral reefs occupy less than one percent of the ocean floor but support approximately 25 percent of all marine species. They are found in tropical and subtropical waters, generally at depths of less than 50 meters where sunlight can penetrate. Coral reefs provide habitat, feeding grounds, and nurseries for thousands of fish and invertebrate species. They also protect coastlines by absorbing wave energy.
The French Revolution began in 1789 with a period of radical political and social transformation in France. The revolution was driven by financial crises, food shortages, and dissatisfaction with the monarchy and aristocracy. The storming of the Bastille on July 14, 1789 is considered a symbolic starting point. The revolution led to the abolition of feudalism, the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, and eventually the execution of King Louis XVI and Queen Marie Antoinette. The period ended with the rise of Napoleon Bonaparte, who took power in a coup in 1799.
Neurons are electrically excitable cells that transmit information throughout the nervous system. The human brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons. Each neuron consists of a cell body, dendrites that receive signals, and an axon that transmits signals to other neurons or to muscles and glands. Neurons communicate through electrochemical signals called action potentials, which travel down the axon. At the synapse, neurotransmitters are released from one neuron and bind to receptors on the next. Different types of neurons include sensory neurons, motor neurons, and interneurons, each with specialized functions.
The Amazon rainforest covers approximately 5.5 million square kilometers and spans across nine countries in South America. It is home to an estimated 390 billion individual trees belonging to around 16,000 species. The forest produces roughly 20 percent of Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis and plays a critical role in regulating the global water and carbon cycles. Scientists estimate that the Amazon contains approximately 10 percent of all species on Earth. The canopy forms a dense layer that intercepts most sunlight, creating a cooler, more humid environment at the forest floor below.
Chess is a two-player strategy board game played on a checkered board with 64 squares arranged in an 8×8 grid. Each player begins with 16 pieces: one king, one queen, two rooks, two knights, two bishops, and eight pawns. The objective is to place the opponent's king in checkmate, a position where it cannot avoid capture. Chess originated in India during the Gupta Empire around the 6th century and spread to Persia, the Arab world, and eventually Europe. Modern tournament rules, including time controls and standardized notation, were established in the 19th and 20th centuries.
Ocean tides are the rise and fall of sea levels caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon and the Sun. The Moon exerts a stronger tidal force than the Sun due to its proximity, despite being much smaller. There are typically two high tides and two low tides per day at most coastal locations. Spring tides, which have greater amplitude, occur when the Sun, Earth, and Moon align. Neap tides, with lower amplitude, occur when the Moon is at a right angle to the Sun relative to Earth. The height and timing of tides vary considerably depending on the location's geography.
The Hubble Space Telescope was launched into low Earth orbit in 1990 and remains operational. It orbits Earth at an altitude of about 547 kilometers and travels at approximately 27,000 kilometers per hour. The telescope has a primary mirror 2.4 meters in diameter and observes primarily in ultraviolet, visible, and near-infrared wavelengths. Hubble has contributed to a wide range of astronomical discoveries including refining measurements of the Hubble constant, observing the deep structure of galaxies, imaging the remains of supernovae, and providing evidence for the accelerating expansion of the universe.
Earthquakes are caused by the sudden release of energy in Earth's lithosphere, creating seismic waves. The point below Earth's surface where the earthquake starts is called the hypocenter or focus. The point on the surface directly above the hypocenter is the epicenter. Earthquakes are measured using the Richter scale or the moment magnitude scale. Most earthquakes occur along tectonic plate boundaries. The Pacific Ring of Fire is a region of high seismic and volcanic activity surrounding the Pacific Ocean. Earthquakes can trigger secondary hazards including tsunamis, landslides, and fires caused by damaged infrastructure.
DNA, or deoxyribonucleic acid, is a molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development, functioning, growth, and reproduction of all known living organisms. DNA is a double helix formed by two strands of nucleotides. Each nucleotide contains one of four nitrogenous bases: adenine, thymine, cytosine, or guanine. Base pairing rules dictate that adenine pairs with thymine and cytosine pairs with guanine. The sequence of these base pairs encodes genetic information. In humans, the complete genome contains approximately 3 billion base pairs organized into 23 pairs of chromosomes located in the cell nucleus.
The Roman Empire at its peak covered approximately 5 million square kilometers. It stretched from Britain in the northwest to Mesopotamia in the east, and from the Rhine and Danube rivers in the north to North Africa in the south. The empire was administered through a complex system of provincial governance, a standing professional army, and an extensive road network of about 400,000 kilometers. Latin served as the administrative language across western territories. The empire is conventionally dated from 27 BCE, when Augustus became the first emperor, to 476 CE when the last western emperor was deposed.
Vaccines work by introducing a harmless component of a pathogen, such as a weakened or inactivated form, a protein subunit, or genetic material, into the body. The immune system responds by producing antibodies and developing immunological memory. If the vaccinated individual is later exposed to the actual pathogen, the immune system can respond more rapidly and effectively. The first vaccine was developed by Edward Jenner in 1796 using cowpox to protect against smallpox. Vaccines have since been developed for numerous infectious diseases including polio, measles, influenza, and hepatitis B.
The Nile River flows northward approximately 6,650 kilometers from its source in the African Great Lakes region to its delta on the Mediterranean Sea. It passes through 11 countries, with its lower reaches running through Sudan and Egypt. The river has two main tributaries: the White Nile, originating in the Great Lakes region, and the Blue Nile, originating in Ethiopia. The annual flooding of the Nile deposited nutrient-rich silt along its banks, making the surrounding land highly fertile and supporting the development of ancient Egyptian civilization. The Aswan High Dam, completed in 1970, now regulates the flow and prevents annual flooding.
Transistors are semiconductor devices used to amplify or switch electronic signals. The first transistor was demonstrated at Bell Labs in 1947. They replaced vacuum tubes in most applications, being smaller, more reliable, and more energy-efficient. A transistor has three terminals: the emitter, the base, and the collector in bipolar junction transistors, or the source, gate, and drain in field-effect transistors. Modern integrated circuits contain billions of transistors on a single silicon chip. The continuous miniaturization of transistors has roughly followed Moore's Law, with the number of transistors per chip approximately doubling every two years.
The Colosseum in Rome is an oval amphitheater built between 70 and 80 CE during the Flavian dynasty. It could hold between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators. The structure measures 188 meters long, 156 meters wide, and 50 meters tall. It was used for gladiatorial contests, public spectacles, animal hunts, executions, reenactments of battles, and dramas. The Colosseum remained in active use for about four centuries. Over the centuries it was damaged by earthquakes and stone robbers who removed materials for use in other buildings. It remains one of the most visited historical monuments in the world.
Antibiotics are compounds that inhibit or kill bacteria. The first antibiotic, penicillin, was discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928 when he observed that the mold Penicillium notatum had contaminated a bacterial culture and inhibited bacterial growth. Antibiotics work through various mechanisms, including disrupting bacterial cell wall synthesis, inhibiting protein synthesis, blocking DNA replication, or disrupting cell membrane function. They are effective against bacterial infections but have no effect on viral infections. Overuse and misuse of antibiotics has led to the emergence of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which poses a significant challenge to global public health.
The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter. It contains between 100 and 400 billion stars and is estimated to contain at least as many planets. The solar system is located about 26,000 light-years from the galactic center in the Orion Arm, one of the galaxy's spiral arms. The Milky Way rotates, with the solar system completing one orbit of the galactic center approximately every 225 to 250 million years. The galactic center contains a supermassive black hole called Sagittarius A*, which has a mass approximately 4 million times that of the Sun.
The Panama Canal is an artificial waterway connecting the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. It stretches approximately 82 kilometers across the Isthmus of Panama. The canal was constructed between 1904 and 1914 by the United States following a failed French attempt in the 1880s. The original lock system was supplemented by a new set of larger locks that opened in 2016. Ships travel through a series of locks that raise and lower vessels to accommodate the difference in water levels. Approximately 14,000 ships pass through the canal each year, carrying about 5 percent of global maritime trade.
The human circulatory system consists of the heart, blood vessels, and blood. The heart is a muscular organ that pumps blood through two circuits: the pulmonary circuit, which circulates blood to and from the lungs for oxygenation, and the systemic circuit, which distributes oxygenated blood to the body and returns deoxygenated blood to the heart. Blood vessels include arteries, which carry blood away from the heart; veins, which return blood to the heart; and capillaries, which are tiny vessels where gas and nutrient exchange occurs with surrounding tissues. An adult human body contains approximately 5 liters of blood.
Volcanoes are openings in Earth's crust through which magma, ash, and gases are expelled. They occur at convergent and divergent plate boundaries, and also above hot spots in the mantle. There are approximately 1,500 potentially active volcanoes on Earth, with around 50 erupting each year. Volcanoes are classified by their shape and eruptive style. Shield volcanoes have broad, gently sloping sides and produce fluid lava flows. Stratovolcanoes, also called composite volcanoes, are steep-sided and erupt explosively. Calderas form when the magma chamber beneath a volcano collapses following a large eruption.
The development of the internet began in the late 1960s with ARPANET, a project funded by the United States Department of Defense. The network initially connected a small number of university and research computers. The transmission control protocol and internet protocol, commonly known as TCP/IP, were developed in the 1970s and became the standard communication protocols. The World Wide Web, a system of interlinked hypertext documents, was proposed by Tim Berners-Lee in 1989 and launched publicly in 1991. Since then, the internet has grown to connect billions of devices worldwide and has transformed communication, commerce, and access to information.
The process of natural selection, described by Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace in the 19th century, is the mechanism by which evolution occurs. Individuals within a population vary in their traits, and some of these variations are heritable. Individuals with traits better suited to their environment tend to survive longer and reproduce more successfully, passing their traits on to subsequent generations. Over many generations, this process can result in the adaptation of populations to their environments and, eventually, in the formation of new species. Natural selection operates on the variation present in populations, which arises through mutation, recombination, and other processes.
The construction of the Egyptian pyramids took place over thousands of years, with the most famous examples built during the Old Kingdom period around 2500 BCE. The Great Pyramid of Giza was built as a tomb for Pharaoh Khufu and originally stood 146.5 meters tall. It is estimated to contain approximately 2.3 million stone blocks weighing between 2.5 and 15 tonnes each. The workforce that built the pyramids was composed of skilled laborers and craftsmen, organized into gangs with specific tasks. Evidence suggests workers were paid in food, beer, and medical care rather than being enslaved.
Atmospheric pressure is the force per unit area exerted by the weight of air above a given point in the atmosphere. At sea level, standard atmospheric pressure is approximately 101,325 pascals or 1 atmosphere. Pressure decreases with altitude as there is less air above. The variation of pressure with altitude follows an approximately exponential relationship. Weather systems are associated with regions of high and low pressure. High-pressure systems generally bring clear, stable weather, while low-pressure systems are associated with clouds, wind, and precipitation. Barometers are instruments used to measure atmospheric pressure.
Linguistic relativity, also known as the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, proposes that the language a person speaks influences their thoughts and perception of the world. The strong version of the hypothesis argues that language determines thought, while the weaker version suggests that language influences thought and certain kinds of reasoning. Empirical studies have examined differences in how speakers of different languages categorize colors, spatial relationships, and numerical quantities. Results have been mixed, with some studies suggesting modest effects of language on cognition, particularly in domains where languages differ systematically in their grammatical structures.
The carbon cycle describes the movement of carbon through Earth's biosphere, geosphere, atmosphere, and hydrosphere. Carbon dioxide in the atmosphere is absorbed by plants during photosynthesis and incorporated into organic compounds. Animals obtain carbon by consuming plants or other animals. When organisms die, decomposers break down organic matter and release carbon dioxide back into the atmosphere. Carbon is also stored in long-term reservoirs including fossil fuels, carbonate rocks, and ocean sediments. Human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels and deforestation, have significantly altered the carbon cycle by increasing the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.
The Silk Road was a network of trade routes connecting East Asia and Southeast Asia with South Asia, Central Asia, West Asia, East Africa, and Southern Europe. It was active from around the 2nd century BCE to the 15th century CE. The routes facilitated not only the trade of goods such as silk, spices, and precious metals but also the exchange of ideas, religious beliefs, artistic traditions, and technologies between diverse civilizations. The routes were used by merchants, pilgrims, monks, soldiers, and nomads. The name Silk Road was coined by German geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen in 1877.
Sonar, which stands for Sound Navigation and Ranging, is a technique that uses sound propagation to navigate, measure distances, communicate, or detect objects on or under the surface of the water. Active sonar emits pulses of sound and listens for their echoes; passive sonar simply listens to sounds emitted by other objects. Sonar was developed during World War I primarily for detecting submarines. Marine animals including bats, dolphins, and whales use a biological form of sonar called echolocation to navigate and hunt in their environments. Modern sonar systems are used in oceanography, fishing, navigation, and military applications.
